The 2017 paper *"Attention Is All You Need"* by Vaswani et al. is arguably the most cited paper in modern AI. Every large language model you use today — GPT, Claude, Gemini — is a Transformer at its core. But what exactly is the attention mechanism doing? Let's build up the math from scratch.

## The Encoder-Decoder Problem

Before Transformers, sequence-to-sequence models used RNNs. The encoder compressed the input into a fixed-size vector, and the decoder unrolled it. The bottleneck was obvious: you're asking a single vector to carry *all* the information across arbitrarily long sequences.

The insight in attention was elegant: **why not let the decoder look back at all encoder states, not just the last one?**

## Scaled Dot-Product Attention

The core operation is deceptively simple. Given queries $\mathbf{Q}$, keys $\mathbf{K}$, and values $\mathbf{V}$:

$$
\text{Attention}(\mathbf{Q}, \mathbf{K}, \mathbf{V}) = \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{K}^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)\mathbf{V}
$$

Where $d_k$ is the key dimension. The scaling factor $\frac{1}{\sqrt{d_k}}$ prevents dot products from growing large in high dimensions, which would push softmax into regions with tiny gradients.

<Callout title="Intuition">
Think of Q as "what I'm looking for," K as "what each token advertises," and V as "what each token actually provides." The dot product $\mathbf{QK}^\top$ measures compatibility, softmax turns it into a probability distribution, and we take a weighted sum of values.
</Callout>

## Multi-Head Attention

A single attention head can only attend to one "type" of relationship. Multi-head attention runs $h$ parallel attention operations with different learned projections:

$$
\text{MultiHead}(\mathbf{Q}, \mathbf{K}, \mathbf{V}) = \text{Concat}(\text{head}_1, \ldots, \text{head}_h)\mathbf{W}^O
$$

where each head is:

$$
\text{head}_i = \text{Attention}(\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{W}_i^Q,\; \mathbf{K}\mathbf{W}_i^K,\; \mathbf{V}\mathbf{W}_i^V)
$$

This lets the model simultaneously attend to syntactic relationships in some heads and semantic relationships in others.

## Positional Encoding

Unlike RNNs, attention is *permutation equivariant* — it doesn't know the order of tokens. The fix is to add positional information directly to the embeddings:

$$
PE_{(pos, 2i)}   = \sin\!\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2i/d_{\text{model}}}}\right)
$$
$$
PE_{(pos, 2i+1)} = \cos\!\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2i/d_{\text{model}}}}\right)
$$

The alternating sine/cosine ensures that positions can be uniquely identified and that relative positions have a consistent geometric relationship.

## Why It Works So Well

Three properties make this architecture unusual:

1. **Constant path length between any two tokens** — attention is $O(1)$ "hops," not $O(n)$ like RNNs. Gradients flow directly.
2. **Parallelizable training** — no sequential dependency, so GPUs can saturate.
3. **Expressiveness** — attention can learn any function of the input tokens; it's the universal approximator analogy for sequences.

The surprising lesson: sometimes the simplest reformulation of a problem — *just look at everything, weigh it* — outperforms years of architectural hand-engineering.
